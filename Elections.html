Addressing the feeling that government has become disconnected from the people often involves looking at "structural" reforms—changes to the rules of the game rather than just the players. Political scientists and legal experts generally group these into several "pillars" designed to restore accountability.

Based on research from nonpartisan organizations like the Brennan Center for Justice, the Brookings Institution, and FairVote, here are the primary proposed solutions to "fix" the system.

1. Reclaiming Representation (Electoral Reform)

The current "winner-take-all" system and gerrymandering often lead to "safe" seats where politicians only fear a primary challenge from the extremes of their own party, rather than the general public.

  • Ranked Choice Voting (RCV): Allows voters to rank candidates by preference. If no one gets a majority, the bottom candidate is eliminated, and their votes go to the voters' second choice. Evidence: A 2024 study showed RCV can increase voter turnout by up to 17% in municipal elections and encourages candidates to seek a broader "consensus" rather than just attacking opponents.
  • Independent Redistricting Commissions: Moving the power to draw voting maps away from politicians and into the hands of independent citizens. This aims to end "gerrymandering," where politicians pick their voters rather than the other way around.

2. Ending the "Money Loophole" (Campaign Finance)

As discussed previously, the goal here is to reduce the dependency of candidates on a tiny fraction of ultra-wealthy donors.

  • Small Donor Matching: The government matches small individual donations (e.g., a 6-to-1 match). This makes a $25 donation worth $175 to a candidate, incentivizing them to spend time talking to regular citizens rather than attending high-dollar fundraisers.
  • The "Stop Secret Money" Act: Many groups advocate for "Dark Money" disclosure laws, requiring any group that spends money on an election to immediately disclose their major donors online.

3. Strengthening Integrity (Ethics & Lobbying)

These reforms aim to prevent the "revolving door" and ensure public officials are not using their office for private gain.

  • Banning Congressional Stock Trading: Proposed laws (like the ETHICS Act) would prohibit members of Congress from owning or trading individual stocks, preventing them from profiting off non-public information they receive in briefings.
  • Closing the Revolving Door: Implementing longer "cooling-off" periods (e.g., 5 years) before a former member of Congress or high-level staffer can register as a lobbyist.
  • Supreme Court Ethics: Creating an enforceable code of conduct for the Supreme Court, including clear rules on gift disclosure and recusal from cases where a conflict of interest exists.

This is why many advocates focus on state-level ballot initiatives, where voters can bypass the legislature to pass these laws directly.

The feasibility of this approach is a paradox: the policies are historically popular among voters, but historically difficult to implement through state legislatures.

As of early 2026, the data shows a massive gap between what the "people" want and what "state actors" are willing to initiate. However, your strategy of excluding state-level campaigns targets the exact pressure point that currently stalls these movements.

2. Is the "State-Led" Path Feasible?

To reach the 34-state threshold for a convention, you have to overcome three major hurdles:

  • The "Self-Preservation" Barrier: State legislators are often "career-track" politicians who hope to run for Congress. They are hesitant to pass laws that would limit their own future fundraising or trading abilities. Your "Federal-Only" carve-out directly solves this.
  • The "Runaway Convention" Fear: Groups like Common Cause and various conservative think tanks actively lobby state legislatures against Article V calls. They argue that once a convention starts, there are no rules to stop delegates from rewriting the entire Constitution (e.g., removing the 2nd Amendment or the right to privacy).
  • Partisan Sorting: Currently, Article V calls are split by party. The "Convention of States" (19 states) is largely Republican-led and focuses on fiscal restraint. "Wolf-PAC" (5 states) is largely Democrat-led and focuses on money in politics. To succeed, a movement must merge these.

3. The "Federal-Only" Unlock

Your idea of excluding state campaigns is the most likely path to success because it creates a "NIMBY" (Not In My Back Yard) ethics reform. State legislators can vote for it to please their constituents (who see 90%+ support) without actually hurting their own "political machines" at home.

[Inference] If a model resolution was drafted that explicitly stated, "This amendment shall apply only to federal offices and shall not infringe upon the power of states to regulate their own elections," it would likely bypass the lobbying of state-level special interests. This could potentially move the current "Money in Politics" count from 5-22 states toward the required 34 much faster.
